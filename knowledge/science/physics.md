# Physics

## Classical Mechanics
Newton's three laws of motion describe how objects move. The first law states that an object at rest stays at rest unless acted on by a net force. The second law defines force as mass times acceleration (F=ma). The third law states every action has an equal and opposite reaction. Energy comes in kinetic form (motion) and potential form (stored). Conservation laws govern both energy and momentum in closed systems. Work is force applied over a distance. Power is work done per unit time.

## Thermodynamics
The four laws of thermodynamics describe heat, energy, and entropy. The zeroth law defines thermal equilibrium. The first law is conservation of energy: energy cannot be created or destroyed, only converted. The second law states entropy (disorder) in a closed system always increases or stays the same. The third law states absolute zero is unreachable. Heat transfers via conduction, convection, and radiation. Temperature is a measure of average kinetic energy of particles.

## Electromagnetism
Electric charge is a fundamental property of matter. Like charges repel, opposite charges attract. Coulomb's law gives the force between point charges. Current is flow of charge; voltage is electric potential difference; resistance opposes current flow. Ohm's law: V = IR. Magnetic fields are produced by moving charges. Maxwell's equations unify electricity, magnetism, and light. Electromagnetic waves include radio, microwave, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, X-rays, and gamma rays.

## Quantum Mechanics
At very small scales, particles behave probabilistically rather than deterministically. The Heisenberg uncertainty principle states that position and momentum cannot both be known exactly simultaneously. Particles exhibit wave-particle duality. The Schrödinger equation describes how quantum states evolve. Quantum numbers describe electron states in atoms. Quantum entanglement and superposition are real, experimentally verified phenomena. Quantum computing exploits superposition and entanglement for computation.

## Relativity
Einstein's special relativity (1905) states that the speed of light is constant for all observers, and that time and space are relative depending on the observer's motion. Mass and energy are equivalent: E=mc². General relativity (1915) describes gravity as the curvature of spacetime caused by mass and energy. GPS satellites require relativistic corrections to remain accurate.

## Nuclear and Particle Physics
Atoms consist of a nucleus (protons and neutrons) surrounded by electrons. Protons and neutrons are made of quarks. The Standard Model describes fundamental particles: quarks, leptons, and bosons. The Higgs boson was confirmed in 2012. Nuclear fission splits heavy atoms releasing large energy. Nuclear fusion combines light atoms releasing even larger energy. Radioactive decay includes alpha, beta, and gamma emission.

## Astrophysics and Cosmology
The Big Bang occurred approximately 13.8 billion years ago. The observable universe is about 93 billion light-years in diameter. Stars form from collapsing gas clouds. Nuclear fusion in stars produces elements up to iron. Elements heavier than iron form in supernovae and neutron star mergers. Black holes form when matter collapses beyond the Schwarzschild radius. Dark matter makes up about 27% of the universe's mass-energy content. Dark energy makes up about 68% and drives accelerating cosmic expansion.
