# Mathematics and Statistics

## Algebra and Equations

Algebra is the branch of mathematics that deals with symbols and the rules for manipulating those symbols. These symbols represent quantities without fixed values, known as variables.

Linear equations take the form ax + b = 0, where a and b are constants and x is the variable. The solution is x = -b/a when a ≠ 0.

Quadratic equations have the form ax² + bx + c = 0. They can be solved using the quadratic formula: x = (-b ± √(b² - 4ac)) / 2a. The discriminant (b² - 4ac) determines the nature of the roots.

Systems of equations can be solved by substitution, elimination, or matrix methods. These are fundamental to modeling real-world scenarios with multiple constraints.

## Geometry and Shapes

Geometry studies the properties and relationships of points, lines, angles, surfaces, and solids.

The Pythagorean theorem states that in a right triangle, a² + b² = c², where c is the hypotenuse. This fundamental relationship connects algebra and geometry.

Circles have area πr² and circumference 2πr. The constant π (approximately 3.14159) appears throughout mathematics and physics.

Polygons are classified by their number of sides: triangles (3), quadrilaterals (4), pentagons (5), hexagons (6), and so on. Regular polygons have equal sides and angles.

## Trigonometry and Angles

Trigonometry relates the angles of triangles to their side lengths using six functions: sine, cosine, tangent, and their reciprocals.

The unit circle definition extends trigonometric functions to all real numbers, not just triangle angles. This is crucial for modeling periodic phenomena.

Key identities include sin²(θ) + cos²(θ) = 1, and the angle addition formulas. These enable simplification and transformation of expressions.

Trigonometry has applications in physics, engineering, navigation, and signal processing.

## Probability Theory

Probability quantifies the likelihood of events occurring, ranging from 0 (impossible) to 1 (certain).

The probability of an event A is P(A) = (number of favorable outcomes) / (total number of possible outcomes), assuming all outcomes are equally likely.

Conditional probability P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B) gives the probability of A given that B has occurred. This is fundamental to Bayesian reasoning.

Independent events satisfy P(A ∩ B) = P(A) × P(B). Understanding independence is crucial for statistical analysis.

## Statistical Distributions

Statistical distributions describe how data values are spread across possible outcomes.

The normal (Gaussian) distribution is bell-shaped and characterized by its mean μ and standard deviation σ. Many natural phenomena approximately follow this distribution.

The binomial distribution models the number of successes in n independent trials, each with probability p of success.

The uniform distribution assigns equal probability to all outcomes in its range. It represents maximum uncertainty within bounds.

## Hypothesis Testing

Hypothesis testing is a statistical method for making decisions about populations based on sample data.

The null hypothesis (H₀) represents the status quo or no-effect scenario. The alternative hypothesis (H₁) represents what we're trying to prove.

The p-value measures the strength of evidence against H₀. A small p-value (typically < 0.05) suggests the data are inconsistent with the null hypothesis.

Type I error occurs when we reject a true null hypothesis (false positive). Type II error occurs when we fail to reject a false null hypothesis (false negative).

## Calculus and Derivatives

Calculus is the mathematics of change, consisting of differential and integral calculus.

The derivative f'(x) measures the instantaneous rate of change of a function at each point. It represents the slope of the tangent line.

Common derivatives: d/dx(xⁿ) = nxⁿ⁻¹, d/dx(eˣ) = eˣ, d/dx(sin x) = cos x, d/dx(ln x) = 1/x.

The chain rule, product rule, and quotient rule are essential techniques for computing derivatives of complex functions.

Derivatives have applications in optimization, physics (velocity and acceleration), economics (marginal cost), and machine learning (gradient descent).

## Matrix Operations

Matrices are rectangular arrays of numbers used to represent linear transformations and systems of equations.

Matrix addition requires matching dimensions. Matrix multiplication is defined when the number of columns in the first equals the number of rows in the second.

The determinant of a square matrix provides information about invertibility and scaling factor of the transformation.

Matrix inversion produces A⁻¹ such that AA⁻¹ = I (identity matrix). Not all matrices are invertible.

Eigenvalues and eigenvectors characterize the fundamental behavior of linear transformations, with applications in physics, engineering, and data science.
