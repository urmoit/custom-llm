# Space and Astronomy

## The Solar System

Our solar system consists of the Sun and all objects bound to it by gravity: eight planets, dwarf planets, moons, asteroids, comets, and meteoroids.

The four inner planets (Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars) are rocky and relatively small. The four outer planets (Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune) are gas and ice giants.

The Sun contains 99.86% of the solar system's mass. It's a medium-sized star powered by nuclear fusion, converting hydrogen into helium.

The asteroid belt lies between Mars and Jupiter. The Kuiper Belt extends beyond Neptune and contains Pluto and other dwarf planets.

## Stars and Their Life Cycles

Stars are massive spheres of hot gas that generate light and heat through nuclear fusion. They form from clouds of gas and dust in space.

A star's life cycle depends on its mass. Low-mass stars live longer (billions of years), while massive stars burn brighter but die younger (millions of years).

Our Sun is about 4.6 billion years old and is roughly halfway through its main sequence life. It will eventually become a red giant, then a white dwarf.

Massive stars end in supernova explosions, leaving behind neutron stars or black holes. These explosions create and distribute heavy elements.

## Galaxies and Clusters

Galaxies are enormous collections of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter held together by gravity. They range from dwarfs with a few billion stars to giants with a hundred trillion stars.

The Milky Way is a barred spiral galaxy about 100,000 light-years across containing 100-400 billion stars. Our solar system is about 26,000 light-years from the galactic center.

Galaxies cluster together in groups and clusters. The Local Group contains the Milky Way, Andromeda, Triangulum, and about 54 smaller galaxies.

Active galactic nuclei (quasars, radio galaxies) are powered by supermassive black holes at galaxy centers, releasing enormous amounts of energy.

## Space Exploration History

Space exploration began in 1957 with the Soviet launch of Sputnik 1, the first artificial satellite. This started the Space Age.

Yuri Gagarin became the first human in space on April 12, 1961, orbiting Earth in Vostok 1.

The Apollo 11 mission landed Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin on the Moon on July 20, 1969. Armstrong's first words: "That's one small step for man, one giant leap for mankind."

The International Space Station (ISS), assembled 1998-2011, represents unprecedented international cooperation. It has been continuously occupied since November 2000.

## Exoplanets

Exoplanets are planets orbiting stars other than our Sun. The first confirmed exoplanet was discovered in 1992 orbiting a pulsar.

The Kepler Space Telescope (2009-2018) discovered thousands of exoplanets using the transit method, detecting dips in starlight as planets pass in front of their stars.

The habitable zone (Goldilocks zone) is the region around a star where conditions might be right for liquid water on a planet's surface—neither too hot nor too cold.

Some exoplanets are very different from our solar system: hot Jupiters orbit close to their stars, super-Earths are larger than Earth but smaller than Neptune, and rogue planets drift through space without stars.

## Telescopes and Observation

Telescopes collect and focus light or other electromagnetic radiation to observe distant objects. They're the primary tool of astronomical research.

Refracting telescopes use lenses to gather light. Reflecting telescopes use mirrors. Most large modern telescopes are reflectors.

Radio telescopes detect radio waves from space, revealing objects invisible in visible light. They can operate day and night and in cloudy conditions.

Space telescopes (Hubble, James Webb) avoid atmospheric distortion and can observe wavelengths blocked by Earth's atmosphere. The James Webb Space Telescope observes primarily in infrared, seeing the most distant galaxies.

## Cosmic Phenomena

Black holes are regions where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape. They form when massive stars collapse.

The event horizon is the boundary beyond which nothing can escape. The size depends on the black hole's mass.

Neutron stars are incredibly dense remnants of supernova explosions, about 1.4 times the Sun's mass compressed into a sphere only about 20 km across.

Dark matter is invisible matter that doesn't emit or absorb light but exerts gravitational influence. It makes up about 27% of the universe.

Dark energy is a mysterious force accelerating the universe's expansion. It comprises about 68% of the universe's total energy content.
