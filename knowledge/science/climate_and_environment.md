# Climate and Environment

## Climate Change Basics
Climate change refers to long-term shifts in global temperatures and weather patterns. While natural factors have always influenced climate, since the mid-20th century human activities have been the dominant driver. The burning of fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas) releases CO₂ and other greenhouse gases (GHGs) that trap heat in the atmosphere — the greenhouse effect. Since pre-industrial times, the global average temperature has risen approximately 1.1–1.2°C. The IPCC (Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change) synthesizes climate science.

## Greenhouse Gases
Key greenhouse gases: CO₂ (carbon dioxide, mainly from fossil fuels and deforestation), CH₄ (methane, from livestock, rice cultivation, and natural gas leaks), N₂O (nitrous oxide, from agriculture and fertilizers), and fluorinated gases (from industrial processes). CO₂ concentration has risen from ~280 ppm pre-industrial to over 420 ppm today — the highest in at least 3 million years. GHGs are measured in CO₂-equivalent (CO₂e) to compare their warming potency.

## Climate Impacts
Rising temperatures cause: more frequent and intense heat waves, altered rainfall patterns, glacier and ice sheet melting, sea level rise (currently ~3.6 mm/year), ocean acidification (from CO₂ absorption), and shifts in ecosystems and species ranges. Extreme weather events including droughts, floods, and hurricanes are intensifying. By 2100, sea levels could rise 0.3–1.0 m or more depending on emissions. Climate change disproportionately harms the world's poorest and most vulnerable populations.

## Renewable Energy
Renewable energy sources do not produce greenhouse gases during operation. Solar photovoltaic (PV) costs have fallen over 90% since 2010 and are now often the cheapest electricity source. Wind power (onshore and offshore) is a major source of clean electricity. Hydropower is the largest renewable electricity source globally. Battery storage (lithium-ion) is key to making intermittent renewables reliable. The energy transition requires electrifying transportation, heating, and industry. Nuclear power produces no GHG during operation but faces concerns about waste and cost.

## Biodiversity and Ecosystems
Earth has entered what scientists call the sixth mass extinction — species are going extinct at 100–1,000 times the natural background rate. Key drivers: habitat destruction, overexploitation, invasive species, pollution, and climate change. Tropical rainforests (Amazon, Congo, Southeast Asia) host over half of all species while covering ~6% of land. Coral reefs cover less than 1% of the ocean floor but support ~25% of marine species. Wetlands filter water and store carbon. Ecosystem services (clean water, air, pollination, food) are estimated to be worth tens of trillions of dollars annually.

## Environmental Policy
The Paris Agreement (2015) aims to limit global warming to 1.5–2°C above pre-industrial levels. Countries submit Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) — voluntary emissions reduction pledges. Net-zero emissions means balancing GHG emitted with GHG removed. Carbon pricing (carbon tax or emissions trading schemes) puts a cost on emissions. The EU's Green Deal aims for climate neutrality by 2050. Deforestation in the Amazon is a major policy and diplomatic issue. Plastic pollution has reached every corner of the ocean; over 8 million tons enter oceans annually.
