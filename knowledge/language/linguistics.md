# Language and Linguistics

## Language Fundamentals
Language is a system of communication using symbols (words, signs) governed by rules (grammar). Phonetics studies sounds; phonology studies sound systems. Morphology studies word structure; syntax studies sentence structure. Semantics studies meaning; pragmatics studies meaning in context. All natural human languages share certain universal properties (language universals). Languages are not static — they evolve continuously through use, contact, and cultural change.

## World Languages
There are approximately 7,000 living languages in the world. Most are spoken by very small communities; half are endangered. The most spoken languages by number of native speakers: Mandarin Chinese (~920M), Spanish (~480M), English (~380M), Hindi (~340M), Arabic (~310M), Bengali (~270M), French (~80M), Portuguese, Russian, Urdu. English is the most widely spoken second language and is the de facto global language of business, science, and aviation.

## Language Families
Languages descended from a common ancestor belong to the same family. Indo-European is the largest family by number of speakers: includes English, Spanish, French, German, Russian, Hindi, Persian. Sino-Tibetan includes Mandarin, Cantonese, Tibetan, Burmese. Afro-Asiatic includes Arabic, Hebrew, Amharic. Niger-Congo is the largest family by number of languages, spoken across sub-Saharan Africa. Austronesian spans from Madagascar to Easter Island to Hawaii. Language isolates (like Basque) have no known relatives.

## Second Language Learning
Second language acquisition (SLA) is the study of how people learn languages beyond their native tongue. Children acquire their first language naturally through immersion and interaction. Adult learners benefit from structured instruction, vocabulary study, grammar understanding, and heavy exposure. Comprehensible input (Krashen) emphasizes exposure to language just beyond current ability. Spaced repetition is highly effective for vocabulary retention. Bilingualism has cognitive benefits including improved executive function and delay of dementia onset. Immersion is the fastest way to achieve fluency.

## Writing Systems
Writing systems encode language visually. Alphabets use symbols for individual sounds (Latin, Cyrillic, Greek, Arabic, Hebrew). Syllabaries use symbols for syllables (Japanese hiragana and katakana, Cherokee). Logographic systems use symbols for words or morphemes (Chinese characters, Egyptian hieroglyphics). Most languages use Latin script. The Phoenician alphabet is the ancestor of most modern alphabets. Cuneiform (Mesopotamia) and Egyptian hieroglyphics are among the oldest writing systems (~3200 BCE).

## Linguistics Concepts
Descriptive linguistics describes how language is actually used; prescriptive linguistics makes rules about how language "should" be used. Code-switching is alternating between languages or dialects in conversation. Dialect vs. language: "a language is a dialect with an army and a navy" (Weinreich). The Sapir-Whorf hypothesis suggests language shapes thought; strong versions are largely discredited, but language does influence perception in subtle ways. Etymology traces word origins. Cognates are words in different languages that share a common origin.
