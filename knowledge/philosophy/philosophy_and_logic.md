# Philosophy and Critical Thinking

## Branches of Philosophy
Philosophy explores fundamental questions about existence, knowledge, values, and reason. Metaphysics studies the nature of reality: what exists, what is time, what is consciousness? Epistemology studies knowledge: what can we know and how? Ethics studies morality: what is right and wrong? Logic studies valid reasoning and inference. Aesthetics studies art and beauty. Political philosophy examines justice, liberty, and governance. Philosophy of mind studies the relationship between mind and body.

## Major Ethical Theories
Consequentialism judges actions by their outcomes; the best action produces the most good for the most people. Utilitarianism (Mill, Bentham) is a form of consequentialism maximizing overall happiness. Deontology (Kant) holds that some actions are intrinsically right or wrong regardless of consequences. The categorical imperative: act only by principles you could universalize. Virtue ethics (Aristotle) focuses on character rather than rules or outcomes. Contractarianism (Rawls) grounds ethics in principles rational people would agree to from a position of ignorance about their own status.

## Logic and Critical Thinking
Deductive reasoning: if premises are true and the argument is valid, the conclusion must be true. Inductive reasoning: premises provide evidence but not certainty for the conclusion. Abductive reasoning: inference to the best explanation. Logical fallacies are errors in reasoning: ad hominem (attacking the person), straw man (misrepresenting a view), false dichotomy (only two options when there are more), appeal to authority, confirmation bias, slippery slope. The scientific method is systematic: hypothesize, predict, test, analyze, conclude.

## Key Philosophers
Socrates used questioning to expose ignorance and seek truth. Plato proposed a world of ideal Forms beyond physical reality. Aristotle systematized logic, ethics, and science. Descartes sought certain foundations ("I think, therefore I am"). Hume argued causation is a mental habit, not a logical necessity. Kant argued the mind actively structures experience. Nietzsche questioned traditional morality and called for creating new values. Wittgenstein argued many philosophical problems arise from misuse of language. Camus explored absurdism: life has no inherent meaning but we can create it.

## Epistemology
Rationalism holds that reason is the primary source of knowledge (Descartes, Leibniz, Spinoza). Empiricism holds that knowledge comes from sensory experience (Locke, Hume, Berkeley). Kant synthesized both: the mind structures raw sensory data using innate categories. Skepticism questions what can be known with certainty. Justified true belief was the traditional definition of knowledge; Gettier problems challenged this. Constructivism holds that knowledge is constructed rather than discovered. Scientific realism holds that successful scientific theories accurately describe reality.

## Philosophy of Mind
The mind-body problem asks how mental states relate to physical brain states. Dualism (Descartes) holds mind and body are distinct substances. Physicalism holds the mind is entirely physical. Functionalism defines mental states by their functional role rather than physical makeup. Qualia are subjective experiential properties ("what it's like" to see red). The hard problem of consciousness asks why there is subjective experience at all. Artificial consciousness and machine sentience remain open questions. Free will debate: determinism, compatibilism, and libertarian free will represent the main positions.
