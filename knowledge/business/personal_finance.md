# Personal Finance

## Budgeting Basics

Budgeting is the process of creating a plan for how you will spend your money. It's the foundation of personal finance.

The 50/30/20 rule is a simple budgeting framework: 50% of income for needs (housing, food, utilities), 30% for wants (entertainment, dining out), and 20% for savings and debt repayment.

Zero-based budgeting assigns every dollar a job, so income minus expenses equals zero. This ensures intentional spending rather than wondering where money went.

Track your expenses for at least one month to understand your spending patterns. Many people are surprised by how much they spend on small, frequent purchases.

Budget apps and spreadsheets can help automate tracking. The key is consistency and regular review.

## Saving Strategies

Saving is paying yourself first. It builds financial security and enables future goals.

Pay yourself first by automatically transferring money to savings before spending on anything else. Treat savings like a non-negotiable expense.

Start small if necessary—even $25 per week adds up to $1,300 per year. The habit matters more than the amount initially.

Name your savings accounts for specific goals (Emergency Fund, Vacation, New Car) to increase motivation and reduce temptation to spend.

Automate your savings through automatic transfers on payday. Out of sight, out of mind works for building wealth.

## Debt Management

Debt is money you owe that must be repaid, usually with interest. Managing it well is crucial to financial health.

The debt avalanche method targets highest-interest debt first, saving the most money on interest over time. Make minimum payments on all debts, then put extra money toward the highest-rate debt.

The debt snowball method targets smallest balances first for psychological wins and motivation. This may cost more in interest but keeps many people motivated to continue.

Credit card debt is typically the most harmful due to high interest rates (often 15-25%+). Pay the full balance every month if possible to avoid interest entirely.

Student loans and mortgages generally have lower rates and may have tax advantages, but still require careful management.

## Credit Scores

A credit score is a number (typically 300-850) that represents your creditworthiness based on your credit history.

Payment history (35%) is the most important factor. Pay all bills on time, every time. Even one late payment can significantly damage your score.

Credit utilization (30%) is the ratio of your current balances to your credit limits. Keep utilization below 30%, ideally below 10%, for the best impact.

Length of credit history (15%) matters. Don't close old accounts unnecessarily. The average age of accounts affects your score.

Credit mix (10%) and new credit inquiries (10%) round out the factors. Having different types of credit can help, but don't open accounts just for this reason.

## Insurance Basics

Insurance protects you from financial losses by transferring risk to an insurance company in exchange for premium payments.

Health insurance covers medical expenses. Understand your deductible (what you pay before insurance kicks in), copay (fixed amount per visit), and coinsurance (percentage you pay).

Auto insurance typically includes liability (damage you cause to others), collision (damage to your car), and comprehensive (non-collision damage like theft or weather).

Renters or homeowners insurance protects your belongings and provides liability coverage. Many people underestimate the value of their possessions.

Life insurance is essential if others depend on your income. Term life insurance is usually the best choice for most people over whole life.

## Investing for Beginners

Investing is using money to buy assets expected to generate returns over time. It's essential for building long-term wealth.

Compound interest is interest earned on both the original principal and accumulated interest. Einstein reportedly called it the eighth wonder of the world. Starting early is the biggest advantage.

Stocks represent ownership in companies. They offer higher potential returns but with higher volatility (price swings).

Bonds are loans to governments or corporations that pay fixed interest. They're generally safer than stocks but offer lower returns.

Index funds and ETFs track market indexes (like the S&P 500) and provide instant diversification at low cost. They're ideal for most beginning investors.

## Retirement Planning

Retirement planning is preparing financially for the time when you stop working. The earlier you start, the easier it is.

401(k) plans are employer-sponsored retirement accounts. Contribute at least enough to get any employer match—it's free money.

Traditional IRAs allow tax-deductible contributions now with tax-deferred growth; you pay taxes on withdrawals in retirement.

Roth IRAs use after-tax money for contributions, but withdrawals in retirement are tax-free. This is advantageous if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket later.

A common rule of thumb is to aim for a retirement nest egg of 25 times your expected annual expenses (the 4% rule). This should allow withdrawals for 30+ years.

## Emergency Funds

An emergency fund is money set aside to cover unexpected expenses or financial emergencies.

Aim for 3-6 months of essential living expenses in your emergency fund. If your income is variable or you're the sole earner, lean toward 6 months.

Keep emergency funds in a readily accessible, low-risk account like a high-yield savings account. Don't invest this money in stocks.

Start with a small goal ($500-$1,000) if you're beginning from zero. Build up to the full amount gradually.

Use the emergency fund only for true emergencies: car repairs, medical bills, job loss—not for sales, vacations, or routine expenses.
