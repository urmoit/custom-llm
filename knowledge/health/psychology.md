# Psychology and Human Behavior

## Cognitive Biases

Cognitive biases are systematic patterns of deviation from norm or rationality in judgment. They affect decision-making and belief formation.

Confirmation bias is the tendency to search for, interpret, and recall information that confirms one's preexisting beliefs. This leads to overconfidence and persistence in错误的 beliefs.

Anchoring bias occurs when people rely too heavily on the first piece of information they encounter when making decisions. This is widely used in pricing and negotiation.

The availability heuristic makes people judge the probability of events by how easily examples come to mind. Recent, dramatic, or emotionally charged events are more easily recalled.

The Dunning-Kruger effect describes how people with low ability at a task overestimate their ability, while experts may underestimate theirs.

## Memory and Learning

Memory is the process by which information is encoded, stored, and retrieved. It's fundamental to learning and identity.

Working memory holds information temporarily for processing, typically 4-7 items for about 20-30 seconds. It's crucial for reasoning and comprehension.

Long-term memory has essentially unlimited capacity and can store information for a lifetime. It includes explicit (declarative) and implicit (procedural) memory.

The spacing effect shows that learning is more effective when study sessions are spread out over time rather than massed together (cramming).

Retrieval practice (testing yourself) strengthens memory more than repeated reading. The act of recall itself modifies the memory.

## Motivation and Goals

Motivation is the process that initiates, guides, and maintains goal-oriented behaviors. It's what causes us to act.

Intrinsic motivation comes from within—personal satisfaction, curiosity, or enjoyment. It tends to produce deeper engagement and creativity.

Extrinsic motivation comes from external factors—rewards, recognition, or avoiding punishment. It's effective for simple, routine tasks.

Goal-setting theory shows that specific, challenging goals lead to higher performance than easy or vague goals. Feedback enhances this effect.

Self-determination theory identifies three innate needs: competence, autonomy, and relatedness. When satisfied, they promote optimal motivation and well-being.

## Social Psychology

Social psychology studies how people's thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are influenced by others.

Conformity is adjusting one's behavior or thinking to match those of other people or a group standard. The Asch experiments demonstrated this powerful effect.

Obedience to authority was demonstrated in Milgram's experiments, where ordinary people would inflict apparent harm on others when instructed by an authority figure.

The bystander effect shows that individuals are less likely to offer help in an emergency when other people are present. Diffusion of responsibility is a key factor.

Group polarization occurs when group discussion leads members to make decisions that are more extreme than the initial inclination of its members.

## Emotional Intelligence

Emotional intelligence (EQ) is the ability to perceive, understand, manage, and use emotions effectively.

Self-awareness is recognizing your own emotions and their effects. It's the foundation of emotional intelligence.

Self-regulation involves managing disruptive emotions and impulses, thinking before acting, and adapting to changing circumstances.

Empathy is understanding others' emotions and perspectives. It's crucial for effective communication and relationships.

Social skills include communication, conflict management, leadership, and building bonds. These skills can be learned and improved with practice.

## Stress and Coping

Stress is the body's response to demands or threats. Chronic stress has serious physical and mental health consequences.

The fight-or-flight response activates the sympathetic nervous system, releasing cortisol and adrenaline. This is adaptive for immediate threats but harmful when chronically activated.

Problem-focused coping tackles the stressor directly through action planning and problem-solving. It works best when you have control over the situation.

Emotion-focused coping manages the emotional response to stress through relaxation, reframing, or seeking support. It's useful when the situation can't be changed.

Resilience is the ability to bounce back from adversity. It can be developed through social support, positive self-talk, and maintaining perspective.

## Decision-Making

Decision-making is the cognitive process of selecting a course of action from multiple alternatives.

Bounded rationality recognizes that people have limited information, cognitive capacity, and time when making decisions. We satisfice rather than optimize.

Prospect theory shows that people value gains and losses differently, being more motivated to avoid losses than acquire equivalent gains (loss aversion).

The paradox of choice suggests that having too many options can lead to decision paralysis and reduced satisfaction with the chosen option.

Intuitive decision-making relies on pattern recognition and experience rather than deliberate analysis. It works well in familiar, predictable situations.

## Personality Types

Personality refers to individual differences in characteristic patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving.

The Big Five personality traits are: Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Neuroticism (OCEAN). These dimensions capture most personality variation.

Extraverts gain energy from social interaction and external stimulation. Introverts prefer quieter, more solitary activities and reflection.

Conscientiousness involves being organized, dependable, and goal-directed. It's a strong predictor of academic and occupational success.

Neuroticism is the tendency to experience negative emotions like anxiety, anger, and depression. High neuroticism is associated with greater risk of mental health issues.
