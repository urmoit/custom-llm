# General Intelligence and Reasoning

## How to Solve Problems
A good problem-solving process: (1) Clearly define the problem — what exactly needs solving? (2) Gather relevant information. (3) Break the problem into smaller sub-problems. (4) Generate multiple possible solutions. (5) Evaluate solutions against criteria. (6) Implement the best option. (7) Review and iterate. This applies to technical, personal, and business problems alike. Rushing to solutions without understanding the problem is the most common mistake.

## Logical Reasoning Patterns
Modus ponens: If P then Q; P is true; therefore Q is true. Modus tollens: If P then Q; Q is false; therefore P is false. Syllogism: All A are B; X is A; therefore X is B. Contrapositive: If P then Q is equivalent to If not-Q then not-P. Proof by contradiction: assume the opposite, show it leads to absurdity. Proof by induction: prove base case, prove that truth for n implies truth for n+1.

## Common Cognitive Biases
Confirmation bias: seeking information that confirms existing beliefs. Availability heuristic: overweighting recent or memorable events. Anchoring: over-relying on the first piece of information received. Dunning-Kruger effect: people with limited knowledge overestimate their competence. Sunk cost fallacy: continuing an endeavor because of past investment rather than future value. Hindsight bias: believing after an event that you predicted it. Framing effect: different presentations of the same information lead to different decisions.

## Research and Information Evaluation
Primary sources are original; secondary sources interpret primaries. Peer-reviewed research has been evaluated by experts before publication. Sample size matters — small samples produce unreliable conclusions. Correlation does not imply causation. P-value below 0.05 is a common but not universal significance threshold. Replication is the gold standard — a finding is more reliable if independently reproduced. Preprint servers (arXiv, bioRxiv) share research before peer review. Wikipedia is a starting point, not a final source.

## Writing and Communication
Good writing is clear, concise, and organized. Structure: introduction (what you'll say), body (say it), conclusion (summarize). Paragraphs cover one main idea. Use active voice over passive voice. Avoid jargon unless writing for specialists. Transitions connect ideas between paragraphs. Revision is essential — first drafts are never the best version. For persuasive writing: state your thesis clearly, provide evidence, address counter-arguments, conclude with a call to action.

## Mathematics in Everyday Life
Percentages: a 20% discount on a $100 item saves $20 (item costs $80). Compound interest: money grows exponentially — $1,000 at 7% annual interest becomes ~$7,600 in 30 years. Probability: if something has a 1-in-6 chance each try, after 6 tries you have a ~67% chance of success at least once (not 100%). Expected value: multiply each outcome by its probability and sum — essential for decision-making under uncertainty. Orders of magnitude: knowing whether something is thousands, millions, or billions apart matters greatly in context.
