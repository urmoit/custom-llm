# World History

## Ancient Civilizations
The earliest civilizations arose in Mesopotamia (Sumer, Babylon, Assyria), Egypt, the Indus Valley, and ancient China around 3500–2000 BCE. These civilizations developed writing, agriculture, cities, law codes (Code of Hammurabi), and monumental architecture (pyramids, ziggurats). Ancient Greece (800–146 BCE) developed democracy, philosophy, mathematics, and theater. The Persian Empire was one of the largest ancient empires. Ancient Rome (753 BCE – 476 CE) built law, engineering, and governance systems that shaped Western civilization.

## Classical Age
The Silk Road connected China, Central Asia, Persia, and Rome, enabling trade of goods and exchange of ideas. The Han Dynasty in China and the Roman Empire represented peak classical civilization around the 1st–2nd century CE. Buddhism spread from India across Asia. The Gupta Empire saw advances in mathematics (including zero), astronomy, and medicine in India. Greek and Roman thought was preserved and extended by Islamic scholars during Europe's early medieval period.

## Medieval Period (500–1500 CE)
After Rome's fall, Europe fragmented into kingdoms. The Byzantine Empire continued Roman culture in the east until 1453. Islam emerged in the 7th century and rapidly spread across the Middle East, North Africa, and Spain. The Crusades (1095–1291) were European military campaigns to capture Jerusalem. The Mongol Empire under Genghis Khan was the largest contiguous land empire in history. The Black Death (1347–1351) killed 30–60% of Europe's population. Medieval Japan saw the rise of feudalism and the samurai class.

## Early Modern Period (1400–1800)
The Renaissance saw a revival of art, science, and humanism in Europe beginning in Italy. The printing press (Gutenberg, ~1440) revolutionized information spread. European exploration led to contact with the Americas (Columbus, 1492), circumnavigation (Magellan, 1519–1522), and eventually colonization. The Protestant Reformation (Luther, 1517) split Christianity in Europe. The Scientific Revolution (Copernicus, Galileo, Newton) transformed understanding of the natural world. The Enlightenment championed reason, individual rights, and democracy.

## Age of Revolutions (1750–1900)
The American Revolution (1776) established the first modern democratic republic. The French Revolution (1789) toppled monarchy and spread ideas of liberty, equality, and fraternity. The Industrial Revolution began in Britain around 1760 and transformed economies globally. Napoleon reshaped Europe and spread revolutionary legal codes. Latin American independence movements freed much of the Americas from European rule by the 1820s. The abolition of slavery progressed in the 19th century.

## 20th Century
World War I (1914–1918) was triggered by assassination and alliance systems; it killed around 20 million. The Russian Revolution (1917) established the first communist state. The Great Depression (1929) caused global economic collapse. World War II (1939–1945) killed 70–85 million people; Nazi Germany committed the Holocaust killing 6 million Jews. The atomic bomb was dropped on Japan in 1945. The Cold War (1947–1991) divided the world between US-led and Soviet-led blocs. Decolonization freed dozens of African and Asian nations. The moon landing occurred in 1969.

## Late 20th and 21st Century
The Soviet Union dissolved in 1991. The internet transformed society from the 1990s. The September 11 attacks (2001) triggered the War on Terror. China's rapid economic growth made it the world's second-largest economy. The global financial crisis hit in 2008. The COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2022) killed millions globally and caused major disruptions. Climate change has emerged as a defining challenge of the 21st century. AI rapid development accelerated in the 2020s.
