# World Geography

## Continents and Oceans
Earth has seven continents: Asia (largest, ~44% of land area), Africa (second largest), North America, South America, Antarctica, Europe, and Australia/Oceania. The five oceans are the Pacific (largest), Atlantic, Indian, Southern, and Arctic. The Pacific Ocean is larger than all land on Earth combined. Major mountain ranges: Himalayas (highest—Mt. Everest at 8,849 m), Andes, Rockies, Alps, Urals, and Caucasus. Major rivers: Nile (longest), Amazon (largest by discharge), Yangtze, Mississippi, Congo.

## Climate Zones
The equatorial zone is hot and humid year-round with dense rainforest. Tropical zones have distinct wet and dry seasons. Subtropical zones feature deserts (Sahara, Arabian, Gobi, Australian Outback). Temperate zones have four seasons and support most of the world's population. Mediterranean climate has hot dry summers and mild wet winters. Continental interiors have extreme temperature ranges. Polar zones are cold year-round; the Arctic is ocean surrounded by land; Antarctica is land covered in ice averaging 2,300 m thick.

## Major Countries Overview
China is the most populous country and world's second-largest economy, covers 9.6 million km². India recently surpassed China as most populous (~1.4 billion), growing rapidly economically. Russia is the largest country by area (17 million km²) spanning 11 time zones. Canada is second largest by area. Brazil is largest in South America and has the world's largest rainforest. Australia is the only country that is also a continent. Nigeria is Africa's most populous country. Indonesia is the world's largest archipelago nation.

## Physical Geography
Plate tectonics drives earthquakes, volcanoes, and mountain formation. The Pacific Ring of Fire is highly seismically active. Erosion by water, wind, and ice shapes landscapes. River deltas, alluvial plains, and river valleys are among the most fertile agricultural regions. Desertification is an expanding global problem. Permafrost covers significant Arctic and subarctic regions and is thawing due to climate change. Coral reefs are among the most biodiverse ecosystems but are severely threatened by warming oceans.

## Human Geography
Population: approximately 8.1 billion people in 2024, concentrated in Asia and Europe. Urbanization: over 55% of the world's population now lives in cities, up from 30% in 1950. Megacities (over 10 million): Tokyo, Delhi, Shanghai, São Paulo, Mexico City, Cairo, Mumbai, Beijing. The demographic transition model describes how birth and death rates change as countries develop. Migration is driven by economic opportunity, conflict, climate, and family. Language families: Indo-European (most spoken globally), Sino-Tibetan, Afro-Asiatic, Niger-Congo, Austronesian.

## Economic Geography
The World Bank classifies countries by income: low, lower-middle, upper-middle, and high income. GDP per capita varies from under $500 (DRC) to over $100,000 (Luxembourg, Monaco). Major trade routes: the Suez Canal connects the Mediterranean to the Red Sea; the Panama Canal connects the Atlantic to the Pacific. The Global South refers to lower-income countries mostly in the Southern Hemisphere and subtropics. BRICS (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa) are major emerging economies. The G7 are the major advanced economies.
