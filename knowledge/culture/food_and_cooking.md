# Food and Cooking

## Cooking Methods

Cooking methods transform raw ingredients into safe, delicious food through the application of heat, chemical processes, or mechanical techniques.

Dry-heat cooking methods include baking (oven), roasting (higher temperature oven), grilling (direct heat from below), broiling (direct heat from above), and sautéing (small amount of oil in a pan). These methods create browning and concentrated flavors.

Moist-heat cooking methods include boiling, simmering, poaching, steaming, and braising. These use water, stock, or steam and are gentler, ideal for delicate foods.

Combination methods like braising and stewing use both dry and moist heat. Food is first seared (dry heat) then cooked slowly in liquid (moist heat), producing tender results from tough cuts.

Frying cooks food in hot fat. Deep frying submerges food completely, while pan-frying uses less oil. The key is maintaining proper oil temperature (typically 325-375°F / 165-190°C).

## Baking Basics

Baking is cooking by dry heat in an oven, primarily used for breads, cakes, pastries, and cookies. It requires precision.

Flour provides structure through gluten formation. All-purpose flour is versatile; bread flour has more protein for chewier texture; cake flour has less for tenderness.

Leavening agents make baked goods rise. Yeast is a living organism that ferments sugars to produce CO₂. Baking powder contains both acid and base and activates with moisture and heat. Baking soda needs an acid to activate.

Fat (butter, oil, shortening) adds flavor, tenderness, and moisture. Solid fats can be creamed with sugar to incorporate air for lift.

Measuring by weight (grams) rather than volume (cups) produces more consistent results in baking.

## Knife Skills

Good knife skills improve efficiency, safety, and the quality of your cooking.

The chef's knife (8-10 inches) is the most versatile and frequently used knife. A quality chef's knife can handle most cutting tasks.

The claw grip protects fingertips: curl fingers under so knuckles guide the knife blade. Keep the knife tip on the cutting board and rock the blade.

Keep knives sharp—a dull knife requires more force and is more likely to slip and cause injury. Hone regularly with a steel and sharpen periodically.

Basic cuts include dice (uniform cubes), mince (very fine), julienne (thin matchsticks), and chiffonade (thin ribbons of herbs or leafy greens).

## Flavor Pairing

Flavor pairing is the art of combining ingredients that complement or enhance each other based on shared chemical compounds or contrasting tastes.

The five basic tastes are sweet, sour (acidic), salty, bitter, and umami (savory). Great dishes balance multiple tastes.

Salt enhances other flavors and reduces bitterness. It's not just about making food taste salty—proper salting makes food taste more like itself.

Acid (lemon juice, vinegar, wine) brightens flavors and cuts through richness. A splash of acid can rescue a dull dish.

Umami-rich ingredients (tomatoes, mushrooms, soy sauce, parmesan, anchovies) add depth and savoriness. They make food taste more satisfying.

Complementary pairings include: sweet + acid (tomato + basil), fat + acid (butter + lemon), salty + sweet (bacon + maple), umami + acid (soy sauce + rice vinegar).

## World Cuisines

World cuisines reflect geography, climate, history, trade, and cultural values. Understanding them broadens culinary horizons.

Italian cuisine emphasizes quality ingredients simply prepared. Key elements: olive oil, tomatoes, garlic, pasta, cheese, fresh herbs, wine. Regional diversity is significant.

Chinese cuisine varies dramatically by region. Cantonese (dim sum, stir-fry), Sichuan (bold, spicy, numbing), and others. Key techniques: stir-frying, steaming, red cooking.

Mexican cuisine is far more than tacos. It features complex moles, fresh salsas, corn as a staple, chiles in countless varieties, and slow-cooked meats.

Indian cuisine uses extensive spice blends (masalas), diverse regional styles, and techniques like tempering spices in hot oil to release flavors.

Japanese cuisine values seasonality, presentation, and umami. Sushi, ramen, tempura, and teriyaki are just a few techniques. Dash stock is fundamental.

## Food Safety

Food safety practices prevent foodborne illness, which affects millions of people annually.

The temperature danger zone is 40-140°F (4-60°C), where bacteria multiply rapidly. Don't leave perish food at room temperature for more than 2 hours.

Cook food to safe internal temperatures: poultry 165°F (74°C), ground meat 160°F (71°C), whole cuts of beef/pork/fish 145°F (63°C), eggs until yolk and white are firm.

Prevent cross-contamination by using separate cutting boards for raw meat and ready-to-eat foods. Wash hands, utensils, and surfaces thoroughly.

Refrigerate leftovers within 2 hours in shallow containers for rapid cooling. Most leftovers keep 3-4 days in the refrigerator.

## Meal Planning

Meal planning is deciding in advance what you'll eat over a period (usually a week). It saves time, money, and reduces stress.

Start by planning just a few dinners and build from there. Don't try to plan every meal perfectly from the start.

Check your pantry, refrigerator, and freezer first, then plan meals around what you already have to reduce waste.

Plan for variety but also plan for leftovers. Cooking once and eating twice is efficient.

Make a shopping list based on your meal plan and stick to it. This reduces impulse purchases and food waste.

## Fermentation

Fermentation is the metabolic process where microorganisms (bacteria, yeast, molds) convert sugars into acids, gases, or alcohol. It preserves food and creates unique flavors.

Lacto-fermentation uses salt-tolerant bacteria to convert sugars into lactic acid. This preserves vegetables and creates sauerkraut, kimchi, and pickles.

Yogurt and kefir are fermented dairy products. The bacteria break down lactose (milk sugar), making these products easier to digest for some lactose-intolerant people.

Kombucha is fermented tea made by adding a SCOBY (symbiotic culture of bacteria and yeast) to sweetened tea. It produces a slightly fizzy, tart beverage.

Fermented foods contain probiotics (beneficial bacteria) that support gut health, which is linked to immune function, mental health, and digestion.
